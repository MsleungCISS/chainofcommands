Chain of Choices

How Injustice Happens · They Called Us Enemy · Grade 8

Before you begin

This really happened.

Between 1942 and 1946, the United States government forced about 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry — two-thirds of them American citizens — out of their homes and into prison camps. No one was charged with a crime. One of them was a four-year-old boy named George Takei, whose memoir They Called Us Enemy you are reading.

In this app you will follow the chain of decisions that made this happen. It was not an accident, and it was not inevitable. At every link, people made choices — and other choices were possible.

Some of what you'll read is difficult. Go at your own pace. Your job isn't to feel bad — it's to understand how injustice happens, so you can recognize the pattern.

Scene 2 · The Spark

One Event, Two Experiences

SUNDAY · DECEMBER 7, 1941
“The Japanese have attacked Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, by air, President Roosevelt has just announced.”Transcript — John Charles Daly, CBS radio bulletin, Dec. 7, 1941

The attack

Japan's navy launched a surprise attack on the U.S. fleet at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. More than 2,400 Americans were killed. The next day, the United States declared war on Japan. Across the country, people were shocked, grieving, and afraid.

In a Japanese American home

Japanese American families heard the same bulletin — with a second fear underneath the first. Japan was now the enemy of their country, and their faces looked like the enemy's. Fathers were questioned by the FBI within days. Neighbors' eyes changed. Nothing they had done had changed at all.

Scene 3 · December 1941 – Spring 1942

Fear Becomes Policy

This is the heart of the story. Nothing here happened automatically. Click each link in the chain of decisions, in order. Notice the arrow between every pair of links: each step made the next step possible — it did not make it necessary.

Counter-evidence

What the Government Knew

Before Executive Order 9066 was signed, the government had already investigated Japanese Americans — twice.

The Munson Report (Nov. 1941), ordered by the White House, found “a remarkable, even extraordinary degree of loyalty” among Japanese Americans and predicted no uprising.

The Ringle Report (Jan. 1942), from Naval Intelligence, concluded Japanese Americans posed no serious threat and that any concerns should be handled individually — not by removing an entire group.

Officials had this information before the order was signed. The chain was a choice, not a reaction.

Scene 4 · Spring 1942

The Order in Action

Executive Order 9066 never used the words “Japanese American.” But on the ground, it worked like this: the Army declared the West Coast a military zone and posted orders on telephone poles. Click the posted notices on the diagram.

The Takei family's morning

In They Called Us Enemy, George Takei remembers soldiers with rifles arriving at his family's Los Angeles home. He was five. His father told him and his brother they were going on a vacation. Each child carried what a child could carry; everything else — the business, the car, the house — stayed behind.

Before the camps: “assembly centers”

Families were first held in temporary — often converted racetracks and fairgrounds, surrounded by fences. The Takei family was taken to the Santa Anita racetrack, where they lived in a converted horse stall. Months later, trains took them onward — to somewhere no one would name.

The Takei family's path

Los Angeles → Santa Anita racetrack → Rohwer, Arkansas → Tule Lake, California.

In 1943 the government gave every adult in the camps a Question 27 asked if they would serve in the U.S. military. Question 28 asked them to “forswear allegiance” to the Emperor of Japan — as if they had ever had any. Answer yes, and you swore loyalty to the country imprisoning you and risked being separated from family. Answer no, and you were labeled disloyal.

It was an impossible choice. George's parents answered “no” to both — and the family was moved to Tule Lake, the harshest camp, a maximum-security center.

Scene 6 · 1945 and after

What Was Lost, What Was Learned

Part A · What was lost

When the camps closed, there was no undo button. Families returned to find homes, farms, and businesses gone — sold off for a fraction of their worth, or simply taken. Many, like the Takeis, started over with almost nothing; George's family lived for a time on Skid Row in Los Angeles. Beyond property, there was that followed people for decades, and a silence in many families — parents who could not bring themselves to talk about the camps, and children who inherited that silence.

Part B · Those who resisted

Remember Scene 3's question — “What could have interrupted the chain?” Some people tried, by name:

Minoru Yasui walked into a police station in 1942 and demanded to be arrested for breaking the , to force a court test. Gordon Hirabayashi refused both the curfew and the removal order. Fred Korematsu refused to report and was arrested. All three took their cases to the Supreme Court — and lost (1943–1944). The Court accepted the government's claim of “military necessity.”

Four decades later, researchers found proof that the government had hidden the Munson and Ringle evidence from the Supreme Court. In the 1980s, through cases, courts threw out all three convictions. They had been right all along — , forty years late.

Discussion prompts per scene
  • Scene 1: Why does the "before" picture matter for understanding an injustice?
  • Scene 2: When something frightening happens to a country, who gets to be "us," and who gets turned into "them"?
  • Scene 3: The government had the Munson and Ringle reports. Why might leaders act against their own evidence? Who was rewarded for it?
  • Scene 4: The exclusion orders said "non-alien" instead of "citizen." What work is that word doing?
  • Scene 5: Why were the questionnaire's two questions impossible to answer safely? Design a fairer version.
  • Scene 6: Yasui, Hirabayashi, and Korematsu lost in 1943–44 and won in the 1980s. Was their resistance a failure in 1944? What counts as "interrupting the chain"?

Language matters

Why we use these words

The government chose soft, official words for what it did. Historians — and this app — use accurate ones. This follows the guidance of Densho and the Japanese American National Museum.

“Evacuation” is what you do to protect people from a flood. These families weren't being protected — they were being imprisoned. When you read a source in this unit, ask: who chose this word, and what does it hide?
